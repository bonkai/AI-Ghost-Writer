*   **“That symbol…”** – This feels weak and relies on the reader to assume Seris is processing something significant. It's a missed opportunity for internal monologue *within* the dialogue – a brief, evocative phrase that hints at the symbol's meaning *before* she explains. Consider something like, “The Obsidian Star…I’ve seen it before…”
*   **Rhys’s repeated questioning (“What is it?” “Firestone Mountains?”)** – While concern is good, Rhys feels a bit *too* reactive. He’s consistently asking for information rather than offering observations or support. Consider having him *state* something he observes about Seris’s reaction, then ask a question. For example: “You went pale. What did you see?” or "That felt…bad. What was it?" This adds depth to his character and avoids him feeling like just an information-gatherer.
*   **“It’s connected to the source of the network,” she said, her voice strained, the words feeling…given, not reasoned.”** – The italicized phrase is excellent direction for the actor/reader, but it doesn’t *show* us. Can you subtly weave that 'given' quality *into* the dialogue itself? Perhaps Seris speaks in fragments, or uses phrasing that feels archaic or not entirely her own.
*   **“We have to go. Before whatever is contained within breaks free.”** – This is functional, but lacks punch. It's a direct statement of the plot requirement. Consider a more emotionally charged line that reveals Seris’s fear or desperation. Something like, “It’s going to *break*. We have to get there *now*.”
*   **Overall:** The dialogue is *competent* but leans towards functional. It delivers information, but doesn’t always *reveal* character or create strong emotional resonance. There's a lot of telling instead of showing through dialogue. The opportunity for subtext and more active participation from Rhys is present.
