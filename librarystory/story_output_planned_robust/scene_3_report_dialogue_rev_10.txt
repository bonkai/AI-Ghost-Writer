*   **Over-reliance on "Projecting" & Internal Monologue:** The entire scene is built around this character "projecting" feelings and not *asking* questions, or even thinking in complete sentences. While establishing a unique communication method is fine, it quickly becomes repetitive and removes any dramatic tension. It feels like a way to avoid actual dialogue. Consider allowing *some* direct questioning, even if the responses aren’t verbal.
*   **Lack of Character Voice:** Because the character is largely communicating through feelings, we don’t get a sense of *who* he is. Is he analytical? Impatient? Fearful? The dialogue (or lack thereof) doesn't reveal personality.
*   **Monotony of Responses:** The Library's responses are consistently vague and atmospheric. While maintaining mystery is good, the constant "shifting energy" and "feeling of vastness" become predictable and lack impact.
*   **"Tell," Don't "Show" Emotion:** The narrative *tells* us he feels disorientation, grief, etc., but we don’t *see* it expressed in any unique way through his "projections." What does *his* sorrow feel like? Is it a frantic, desperate plea, or a quiet, resigned acceptance?
*   **Repetitive Structure:** The pattern of "He projected… The Library responded with a feeling…" is repeated ad nauseam. This creates a predictable rhythm that dulls the impact of each revelation.
*   **Infodump Potential:** The initial explanation of being a "resonance" and a "fragment of awareness" feels awkwardly delivered, even though it's not *spoken*. It’s a large amount of exposition dropped on the reader without being earned through interaction or discovery. Consider revealing this information more gradually.
*   **Indirect Characterization Opportunity Missed:** The Library is a character, too! Its responses could be subtly shaped to reflect its own personality or agenda. Is it benevolent, indifferent, or manipulative? We don't get a sense of this.
