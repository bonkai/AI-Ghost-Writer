The air tasted of ash and regret. Not the sharp, acrid tang of recent fire, but a stale, lingering residue, as if the city itself had been slowly burning for centuries. Kaelen drifted, a silent observer, through a narrow alleyway choked with refuse. Above, the sky was a bruised purple, perpetually obscured by the towering, soot-stained structures of Verenhal. Buildings leaned inwards, as if conspiring to swallow the meager sunlight.

He focused on a woman huddled in the shadows, her face hidden beneath a patched hood. She wasn't begging, merely *existing* in the alley’s gloom, a silent indictment of the city’s wealth disparity. Around her, the stone walls were covered in faded murals – depictions of vibrant forests, soaring birds, and smiling faces – now defaced with the stark, angular symbols of the Iron Church. 

A heavy tread echoed from the alley’s mouth. Two Inquisitors, clad in polished steel and crimson robes, marched past, their faces impassive, their eyes scanning for any sign of dissent. One carried a heavy, ornate hammer, the symbol of the Church’s authority. The other held a small, leather-bound book – a registry of forbidden texts and heretical individuals. 

“Another day, another sin to purge,” the Inquisitor with the hammer grunted, his voice devoid of emotion.

“Patience, Brother Marius,” the other replied, his voice smoother, more calculating. “The rot runs deep in Verenhal. We must be thorough.”

They passed the huddled woman without a glance. Kaelen felt a prickle of… something. Not empathy, not exactly. He was still adjusting to this existence, this inability to *act*. But the woman's quiet desperation resonated with a hollow ache within him.

He drifted towards a crumbling archway, leading to a small courtyard. In the center stood a weathered statue, once depicting a benevolent goddess of knowledge, now defaced and draped with Church banners. Around the base, he noticed small, meticulously arranged piles of stones – offerings, perhaps? Or acts of quiet rebellion?

A young boy, no older than ten, darted into the courtyard, clutching a small, tarnished locket. He glanced nervously over his shoulder, then quickly concealed the locket beneath a loose stone in the courtyard wall. Kaelen focused on the locket. It wasn’t gold or silver, but a dull, grey metal, etched with intricate carvings – symbols that felt… familiar. Ancient. 

Suddenly, a voice boomed from the archway. “Boy! What are you doing here?”

An Inquisitor, younger than the others, stood silhouetted against the fading light. He wasn’t wearing the full regalia of the order, but a simple, dark tunic. He carried no weapons, only a scroll and a quill. 

The boy froze, his face pale with fear. “N-nothing, sir. Just… passing through.”

The Inquisitor approached, his eyes narrowed. “Let me see what you’re hiding.”

The boy hesitated, then slowly extended his hand, revealing a handful of wildflowers. A pathetic offering.

“Lies,” the Inquisitor said, his voice cold. He seized the boy’s arm, forcing him to drop the flowers. “What were you concealing near the statue?”

The boy remained silent, his eyes fixed on the ground.

“Speak!” the Inquisitor demanded, his grip tightening.

Kaelen felt a surge of… frustration. He wanted to intervene, to shield the boy, but he was powerless. A ghost in a dying city.

The boy finally whispered, “Just a locket, sir. It belonged to my mother.”

The Inquisitor snatched the locket from the boy’s hand, examining it with a disdainful expression. “Heretical trinkets. Symbols of the Old Gods. Your mother was a follower of the forbidden ways.”

He raised his hand, preparing to strike.

Before the blow could land, a voice called out from the alleyway. “Brother Lucian. Is there a problem?”

The Inquisitor lowered his hand, turning to face the newcomer. It was the smoother, more calculating Inquisitor from earlier.

“This boy possesses a relic of the Old Faith,” Lucian explained, his voice tight with anger.

The older Inquisitor took the locket, examining it with a practiced eye. A flicker of recognition crossed his face. “Interesting,” he murmured. “This is a relic of the Archivists. A forgotten order dedicated to preserving the lost lore of Eldoria.”

He turned back to the boy, his expression unreadable. “Take him to the Citadel. He will be questioned. And this…” He held up the locket. “…will be added to our collection.”

As the Inquisitors led the boy away, Kaelen lingered in the courtyard, a silent witness to the city’s slow decay. The locket. The Archivists. The forgotten lore. Fragments of a past that Verenhal desperately tried to bury. He felt a growing sense that he wasn't just a ghost, but a piece of that lost past, somehow drawn back to this dying city. And he had a feeling his arrival wasn’t accidental.
