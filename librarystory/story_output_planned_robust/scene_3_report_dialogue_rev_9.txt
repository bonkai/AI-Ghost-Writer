*   **Repetitive Structure:** The dialogue relies *heavily* on question/response, question/response. While this establishes the power dynamic and his disorientation, it becomes predictable and lacks nuance after a while. Consider internal monologue or shifts in *how* he tries to communicate (beyond just asking more questions).
*   **The "Presence" Voice:** The library's responses are consistently vague and philosophical. While fitting for an ancient entity, it feels a bit static. Injecting *some* personality, even subtle shifts in tone beyond "ancient and vast," would make it more engaging. The "dry, rustling sound" is a good start, but build on that.
*   **"Tell, Don't Show" with Emotion:** The scene *tells* us he's desperate, urgent, or filled with grief. The dialogue itself doesn't always *demonstrate* that. For example, instead of "the question laced with urgency," *show* that urgency through fragmented thoughts or a more frantic projection of his awareness.
*   **Limited Character Voice (Protagonist):** His internal thoughts bleed into his projections to the Library. While understandable given the circumstances, it makes him feel a bit passive. Give him moments where he attempts to *manipulate* the Library, or expresses frustration/anger more directly, even if it’s ineffective.
*   **Subtext Opportunity:** The constant testing feels stated rather than revealed. Could the Library subtly *lead* him towards certain questions, or respond to his emotional state in ways that hint at its motives?
*   **"Remember" as a Crutch:** The repeated use of "Remember" is becoming a bit of a mantra. Explore other ways to convey the importance of his past without relying on that single word.
*   **Indirect Characterization - Missing:** We learn almost nothing about *who* this man was as a reader, or what kind of stories he enjoyed. Hints of this would enrich the scene and give the "Remember" prompts more weight.
