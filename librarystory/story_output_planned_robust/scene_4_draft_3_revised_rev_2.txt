## Scene 4 - Draft 3

The air tasted of ash and regret. Rain threatened, clinging to the oppressive stillness of the courtyard. Kaelen drifted through the crumbling stone, a prickling awareness settling over him – not with witnessing, but with *being* witnessed. A sensation he hadn’t experienced since… before.

A boy, barely ten summers old, darted into the space, clutching something hidden. He moved like a cornered animal, glancing back with desperate hope. He pressed a tarnished locket beneath a loose stone at the base of a weathered statue, a forgotten deity barely discernible beneath layers of grime. The boy’s fingers trembled as he covered it, a fleeting prayer on his lips.

Kaelen’s attention sharpened. The locket wasn’t gold or silver, but a dull, grey metal, etched with intricate carvings. Symbols that… stirred something within him. Not memory, but a potential echoing in his own being. Fragments of a forgotten language, a lost history, flickered at the edge of his awareness, like a half-remembered dream struggling to surface. He felt a phantom ache in a place he no longer possessed – a hand, perhaps, once skilled at deciphering such things.

“Boy!” The voice, sharp and commanding, sliced through the silence. Lucian, a young Inquisitor, stood framed in the archway, his face obscured by shadow, radiating authority. The shadow seemed to *cling* to him, not fall *on* him.

The boy froze, his face draining of color. “N-nothing, sir. Just… passing through.”

Lucian advanced, his boots crunching on the gravel. “Let me see what you’re hiding.” His voice wasn't a demand, but an accusation already delivered.

The boy hesitated, then extended a trembling hand, revealing a handful of wildflowers – a pathetic, desperate offering. The scent, faint but present, seemed to momentarily confuse Lucian.

“Lies.” Lucian’s voice was chipped ice. He seized the boy’s arm, forcing him to drop the flowers. “What were you concealing near the statue?”

A coldness settled over Kaelen, mirroring the boy’s fear. He couldn't intervene, but the air around Lucian shimmered, a momentary drop in temperature, a subtle disorientation crossing the Inquisitor’s face. Barely noticeable, but enough to make Lucian stumble, a flicker of annoyance crossing his features.

The boy whispered, “Just a locket, sir. It belonged to my mother.”

Lucian snatched the locket, examining it with disdain. “Heretical trinkets. Symbols of the Old Gods. Your mother followed the forbidden ways.” He turned the locket over and over, as if searching for a flaw, a justification for his anger.

He raised his hand, preparing to strike.

“Brother Lucian. Is there a problem?” Valerius, the smoother, more calculating Inquisitor, arrived, defusing the tension with his mere presence. Valerius didn't *walk* into the courtyard, he *glided*, his movements unsettlingly fluid.

“This boy possesses a relic of the Old Faith,” Lucian explained, his voice tight with anger.

Valerius took the locket, his fingers tracing the carvings with a gloved touch. His expression remained unreadable, but a flicker of recognition widened his eyes. “Interesting,” he murmured. “A relic of the Archivists. A forgotten order dedicated to preserving the lost lore of Eldoria.” He held the locket up to the light, studying it with a scholar’s curiosity, not a zealot’s condemnation.

He turned back to the boy, his voice measured. “Take him to the Citadel. He will be questioned. And this…” He held up the locket. “…will be added to our collection.”

As the Inquisitors led the boy away, Kaelen lingered. The rain began to fall, washing over the stone. He reached out, his spectral hand passing through the cold, damp stone of the statue. A faint resonance pulsed beneath his touch, a whisper of power. It wasn't a memory, but a pull, a connection. He focused on the statue, on the lingering energy, and a fragmented image flickered in his mind: a vast library, filled with ancient scrolls. The Archivists. Their purpose. Their fate. A sense of profound loss washed over him, a grief that wasn’t his own, yet felt intimately familiar.

He wasn't merely an observer. He was bound to this place, to this history. And the locket wasn’t just a relic. It was a key. A key to unlocking a forgotten past, and perhaps, to understanding his own purpose. The rain fell harder, washing away the dust of ages, revealing, beneath the grime, a hidden truth. He felt a growing certainty: the Archivists hadn't simply *preserved* knowledge. They had *protected* something. And whatever that something was, it was still here, waiting to be found.
