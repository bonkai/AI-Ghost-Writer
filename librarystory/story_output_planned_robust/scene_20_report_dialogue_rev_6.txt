*   **Over-Explanation/Internal Monologue Disguised as Dialogue:** Several lines feel like Hemlock *telling* us his feelings/history rather than *showing* them through interaction. For example, “I believed in the purity of the Light…I thought I understood order.” This could be conveyed more subtly through his actions, reactions to the mosaic, or brief, evocative statements. Similarly, Seris’s internal state (“The crimson stain on the sketch felt like a brand…”) is often *stated* rather than *revealed* through dialogue or action.
*   **Repetitive Emphasis:** The constant reiteration of the danger of the knowledge and the Church wanting to bury it feels heavy-handed. We *get* it. Trust the audience. Subtlety will heighten the tension.
*   **Touch as Exposition:** Both instances of Hemlock touching Seris (arm brush, hand on pack) are immediately followed by explanation of *why* he did it (fear, grounding). Let the touch *be* the moment, and let Seris *react* to it, allowing the reader to infer his motivation.
*   **Hemlock's Voice:** While generally good, Hemlock sometimes slips into overly poetic/philosophical language ("We tell the stories. And we protect the embers of what remains."). It feels slightly out of character for someone described as a meticulous cataloger who was punished for questioning. Ground it more in his former profession – perhaps a precise description of the lost knowledge, or a concern for preserving the texts.
*   **Seris’s Lack of Probing:** Seris mostly *receives* information. She needs to *drive* the conversation more. Ask specific questions about the memories, the Order’s methods, or the Sunstone Bloom itself. This will reveal her character (curiosity, determination) and create more dynamic interaction.
*   **“What can we do?” feels weak:** After all the build-up, this feels like a passive question. Something more active and determined would fit Seris’s newfound resolve better.
* **Indirect Characterization Opportunity:** The detail about Hemlock being a meticulous cataloger is great. Lean into this more. Perhaps he categorizes the books in a specific, obsessive way, or corrects Seris’s phrasing when she speaks about the lost knowledge.
