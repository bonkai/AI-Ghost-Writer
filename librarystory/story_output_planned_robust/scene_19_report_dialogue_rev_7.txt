*   **Repetitive Acknowledgements:** The scene relies heavily on "shared understandings" and "silent acknowledgements" passing between Seris and Kaelen. While *some* is effective, it happens too frequently. Consider *showing* their connection through action or subtle reactions instead of *telling* us they understand each other. For example, instead of "a fleeting connection, a shared understanding," perhaps Seris unconsciously mirrors Kaelen's posture, or her breathing slows to match his.
*   **Kaelen's Exposition:** Kaelen does a lot of explaining. While necessary to convey information, it feels a bit info-dumpy. Specifically, "The Order didn’t just forbid magic. They forbade *remembering*. They feared the past." This could be revealed more organically through dialogue *about* the Order’s actions and consequences, rather than a direct statement of their motivations. Perhaps Seris asks a question that *leads* Kaelen to reveal this information.
*   **Seris’s Reactions – Passive:** Seris largely *reacts* to Kaelen's information. Her questions ("Lost how?" "This isn’t about a rare healing plant, is it?") are functional but lack a strong individual voice. Consider giving her more proactive lines – lines that reveal her thoughts, fears, or strategic thinking.
*   **"We have to be careful" – Cliché:** This line is a bit weak and doesn’t add much. It's a placeholder for more specific action or internal thought. What *specifically* is she careful about? Can that be implied or shown?
*   **Subtext Opportunity – Valerius:** The dialogue about Valerius is direct. While clarity is good, consider adding a layer of subtext. Perhaps Kaelen doesn't explicitly *say* Valerius suspects them, but describes Valerius's behavior in a way that implies suspicion. (“He’s been…observant tonight.”)
*   **Metallic Scent – Untied:** The metallic scent is a great atmospheric detail, but the dialogue doesn’t connect to it. A quick reaction from either character ("Do you smell that?") could heighten the tension and make the detail more impactful.
