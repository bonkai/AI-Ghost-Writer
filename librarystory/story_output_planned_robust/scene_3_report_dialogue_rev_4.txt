*   **Repetitive "Projection" of Thought:** The constant use of "projecting" thought feels clunky and tells us *how* he's communicating rather than letting the dialogue *be* the communication. It's a narrative shortcut that weakens the impact. Consider removing it entirely and simply writing the questions/statements as if they are being directly communicated.

*   **Voice Consistency:** The voice of the Library is good overall, evoking age and vastness. However, the shift from a dry chuckle to a "soothing balm" tone feels a bit abrupt. While it's not *wrong*, it lacks nuance. Perhaps a slight echo of the dryness even in the gentler moments would maintain consistency.

*   **"Want" as a Plot Point:** The Library's dismissal of "want" is a strong thematic element. But it's stated a little too directly. Instead of *telling* us it doesn’t "want," show it through its actions or lack thereof. For example, the Library could respond to his question with another question, or simply ignore the implied desire for an answer.

*   **"Soon" – Too Cryptic:** The final "Soon" is *almost* effective, but it feels a little too much like a cheap thriller trope. It needs more weight or context. Consider what the Library is "soon" going to do, and subtly hint at that action *before* the word is spoken.

*   **Internal Dialogue vs. External Dialogue:** The line between his internal thoughts and the external dialogue with the Library is sometimes blurred. While a degree of ambiguity is okay, make sure the reader can clearly distinguish between what he’s thinking and what he’s *saying* (even if "saying" is telepathic).

*   **Overuse of Adverbs/Descriptive Tags:** Phrases like "dry, rustling sound" and "gentle, a soothing balm" are telling, not showing. Trust the dialogue itself to convey the Library's tone.

*   **"Welcome Home" – Mixed Signals:** The "Welcome Home" is immediately undercut by "growing unease." This creates a jarring effect. Either lean into the warmth and let the unease build more gradually, or delay the welcome until the unease is more established.
