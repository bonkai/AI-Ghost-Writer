The chill wasn’t from the stone walls of the Atheneum, nor the damp air rising from the crumbling city below. It settled deep in Seris’s bones, a premonition that tightened her chest. She traced the rim of her chipped ceramic mug, the lukewarm herbal tea doing little to warm her.

Across the small, cluttered table, Rhys meticulously cleaned his climbing gear, the rhythmic rasp of steel on leather a fragile counterpoint to the silence. He hadn't spoken since Valerius’s departure, and the tension radiating from him was almost palpable.

A new shadow fell across the table, but this one wasn’t accompanied by the scent of sandalwood. It smelled of old parchment, dust, and something else…something akin to ozone.

“A visitor,” Kaelen’s voice whispered, a rustling in the air that seemed to vibrate the very pages of the surrounding books. “And not a welcome one.”

Before Seris could respond, a figure materialized in the archway leading deeper into the Atheneum. Tall and gaunt, clad in the austere black robes of the Order of the Silver Flame, he carried himself with an unnerving stillness. His face was pale, almost translucent, framed by close-cropped silver hair. A single silver ring, etched with a stylized flame, adorned his left hand.

“Seris Valerius,” he said, his voice a dry rasp that echoed in the cavernous space. “And Rhys Thorne. I am Inquisitor Marius.”

Rhys paused in his work, his gaze sharpening. He didn’t offer a greeting.

“We know of your…interests,” Marius continued, his eyes sweeping over the scattered maps and botanical sketches that covered their worktable. “Your recent inquiries regarding the Sunstone Bloom, your planned expedition to Firepeak. Such pursuits require…authorization.”

“Authorization?” Seris echoed, carefully keeping her voice level. “We are scholars, Inquisitor. We seek knowledge, not permission.”

Marius’s lips thinned. “Knowledge can be a dangerous weapon in the wrong hands. The Order exists to safeguard Eldoria from…uncontrolled curiosity.” He gestured with a gloved hand. “I have reviewed your records. Your…associates are…unconventional. A former mercenary, a disgraced herbalist…and a patron with…questionable allegiances.”

Seris clenched her jaw. He was referring to Old Man Hemlock, the eccentric apothecary who’d provided them with rare reagents. “Our associates are skilled in their respective fields,” she said, her voice tight. “And their backgrounds are irrelevant to our research.”

“Irrelevant?” Marius raised a pale eyebrow. “Everything is relevant, Scholar. Especially when it concerns the ancient lore of Eldoria. The Sunstone Bloom is not merely a botanical curiosity. It is a relic of the First Age, imbued with…memories. Memories that some would prefer remain buried.”

Kaelen’s voice, a frantic whisper, filled Seris’s mind. *He knows. He knows more than he lets on.*

Rhys finally spoke, his voice low and dangerous. “What do you want, Inquisitor?”

Marius’s gaze fixed on Seris. “I want to understand your motives. Your obsession with a flower that blooms only in the most treacherous of locations. Your disregard for the dangers of Firepeak. And I want to be certain that your research does not…unearth something that should remain lost.”

He stepped closer, his eyes boring into Seris. “Tell me, Scholar. What are you *truly* seeking on Firepeak Mountain?”

Seris met his gaze, refusing to flinch. “The truth, Inquisitor. The same thing you claim to seek.”

Marius’s lips curved into a cold smile. “A convenient answer. But I suspect…you are not being entirely truthful.” He paused, then added, his voice dropping to a silken whisper, “I will be watching you, Seris Valerius. Every step you take. And should I find evidence of…heresy…or forbidden knowledge…you will face the full wrath of the Order.” He turned and strode away, his black robes disappearing into the shadows.

The silence that followed was thick with dread. Rhys finally broke it, his voice grim. “He’s not here to offer assistance.”

Kaelen’s voice, a desperate plea, echoed in Seris’s mind. *We need to leave. Now.* But Seris knew, with a chilling certainty, that they couldn't. Not yet. The Inquisitor had made it clear: they were being watched. And running would only confirm his suspicions. They were trapped, caught in a web of intrigue and ancient secrets, with a dangerous enemy closing in.
