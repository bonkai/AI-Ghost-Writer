## Scene 4 - Draft 3

The air tasted of ash and regret. Rain threatened, clinging to the oppressive stillness of the courtyard. Kaelen drifted through the crumbling stone, a prickling awareness settling over him – not with witnessing, but with *being* witnessed. A sensation he hadn’t experienced since… before. It wasn't observation; it was a resonance, a faint echo of attention directed *at* him.

A boy, barely ten summers old, darted into the space, clutching something hidden. He moved with the skittishness of a trapped bird, his gaze darting between the shadowed corners. He pressed a tarnished locket beneath a loose stone at the base of a weathered statue – a forgotten deity barely discernible beneath layers of grime. His fingers trembled as he covered it, a silent plea forming on his lips.

Kaelen’s attention sharpened. The locket wasn’t gold or silver, but a dull, grey metal, cool to the imagined touch. Its carvings weren’t ornamental, but precise, almost mathematical in their complexity. He felt a phantom ache – not in a hand he once possessed, but a *lack* of sensation where skilled fingers should be, the ghost of muscle memory.

“Boy.” The voice, sharp and controlled, sliced through the silence. Lucian, a young Inquisitor, stood framed in the archway, his face largely obscured by shadow. The shadow didn’t simply *fall* on him; it seemed to *gather*, to cling to his form, almost *feeding* on the dim light.

The boy froze, his face draining of color. “N-nothing, sir. Just… passing through.” He didn't meet Lucian’s gaze.

Lucian advanced, his boots crunching on the gravel. He didn’t demand, he *implied*. “Let me see what you’re concealing.” His voice held a quiet certainty, an accusation already delivered.

The boy hesitated, then extended a trembling hand, revealing a handful of wildflowers – a pathetic, desperate offering. The scent, faint but present, seemed to momentarily… distract Lucian. A flicker of something unreadable crossed the Inquisitor’s features – a momentary softening, quickly masked.

“Lies.” Lucian’s voice was chipped ice, but carefully measured. He seized the boy’s arm, forcing him to drop the flowers. “What were you concealing near the statue?”

A coldness settled over Kaelen, mirroring the boy’s fear. He couldn't intervene directly, but the air around Lucian shimmered – a momentary drop in temperature, a subtle disorientation crossing the Inquisitor’s face. Barely noticeable, but enough to make Lucian stumble, a flicker of annoyance crossing his features. It wasn't a disruption; it was a *resistance*, a momentary friction against the Inquisitor's will.

“This boy possesses a relic of the Old Faith,” Lucian explained, his voice tight with barely suppressed anger. He didn’t shout; he *reported*, as if delivering evidence to a silent judge.

“Interesting.” Valerius took the locket, his fingers tracing the carvings with a gloved touch. His expression remained unreadable, but a flicker of recognition widened his eyes. “A relic of the Archivists. A forgotten order dedicated to preserving the lost lore of Eldoria.” He held the locket up to the light, studying it with a scholar’s curiosity, not a zealot’s condemnation. “They weren't merely preservers of lore, Lucian. They were its protectors.”

He turned back to the boy, his voice measured and calm. “Take him to the Citadel. He will be questioned. And this…” He held up the locket. “…will be added to our collection. See it cataloged with particular care. I want a full report on its composition and any accompanying symbols.”

As the Inquisitors led the boy away, Kaelen lingered. The rain began to fall, washing over the stone. He reached out, his spectral hand passing through the cold, damp stone of the statue. A faint resonance pulsed beneath his touch, a whisper of power. It wasn't a memory, but a pull, a connection. He focused on the statue, on the lingering energy, and a fragmented image flickered in his mind: a vast library, filled with ancient scrolls, protected by intricate wards. The Archivists. Their purpose. Their fate. A sense of profound loss washed over him, a grief that wasn’t his own, yet felt intimately familiar.

He wasn't merely an observer. He was bound to this place, to this history. And the locket wasn’t just a relic. It was a key. A key to unlocking a forgotten past, and perhaps, to understanding his own purpose. The rain fell harder, washing away the dust of ages, revealing, beneath the grime, a hidden truth. He felt a growing certainty: the Archivists hadn't simply *preserved* knowledge. They had *protected* something. And whatever that something was, it was still here, waiting to be found. It wasn't hidden; it was *guarded*. He felt a subtle shift in the energy around the statue, a tightening of the wards, as if the locket’s removal had awakened something dormant. The Archivists weren’t just gone. They were waiting. And he, somehow, was connected to their vigil.
