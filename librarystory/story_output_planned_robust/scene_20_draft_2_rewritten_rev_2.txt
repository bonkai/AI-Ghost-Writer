The scent of woodsmoke and damp stone clung to the air, thick and cloying. Seris knelt before a crumbling hearth, tracing the chipped mosaic of a winged figure – a forgotten goddess, judging by Rhys’s unearthed texts. The stone felt cold, a chill that seeped into her bones. A flicker of movement in the shadows made her hand instinctively tighten around the hilt of her dagger.

“You seek to understand why I strayed from the Light, child?” A brittle voice echoed, laced with regret. Old Man Hemlock emerged, pacing like a caged animal, his movements jerky and agitated. “Some doors are best left unopened. Some memories…unremembered.” He stopped abruptly, glancing towards the tunnel entrance. “They have long ears, the Order. Even in exile, we are not truly alone.”

Seris didn’t turn, her senses on high alert. “I seek to understand *you*,” she replied, her voice steady despite the tremor in her hands. “Why risk everything for these…heretical texts?”

Hemlock ran a hand over the cold stone, a haunted look in his eyes. “I was a Scholar, once. Dedicated. Zealous. I believed in the purity of the Light.” He paused, his gaze fixed on the mosaic. “Then I found the Archives. Hidden beneath the Grand Cathedral. Records the Order had…buried.”

Seris leaned forward, her breath catching. “What kind of records?”

“Records of the Before-Times.” Hemlock’s voice dropped to a rasping whisper. “A world steeped in magic, not the sanctioned blessings we offer now, but a raw, untamed force.” He gestured towards a faded section of the mosaic, depicting a swirling vortex. “I saw images…cities built of shimmering crystal, powered by the very lifeblood of the earth. Gardens that bloomed with bioluminescent flora. And records of *why* that magic was suppressed.” He grabbed Seris’s arm, his grip surprisingly strong. “They didn’t just fear the magic, child. They feared what people *remembered* with it.”

He released her, stepping back into the shadows. “The Order deemed those memories dangerous. They didn’t destroy them. They *buried* them. And built the Church upon the grave, a monument to forgetting.” A scraping sound echoed from the tunnel entrance. Hemlock whirled around, eyes wide with fear. “They send observers, you know. To test the edges of our silence.”

Seris felt a chill crawl down her spine. “You’re saying the Church…suppresses knowledge?”

“Erodes it,” Hemlock corrected, his voice strained. “Not just taking what is known, but the *capacity* to remember.” He touched the swirling vortex on the mosaic. “These depict the ‘Memory Wells’ – places where the Order siphoned away the collective memories.” He glanced nervously towards the tunnel entrance. “They don’t want people asking ‘why.’”

“I questioned it,” Hemlock continued, his voice barely a whisper. “I dared to ask *why*. And for that, I was branded a heretic. Stripped of my rank. Exiled.” He stumbled back, clutching his chest. “They said I was ‘unstable.’ ‘A danger to the faithful.’”

“But you saved these texts,” Seris said, gesturing to the stacks of crumbling books and scrolls.

Hemlock’s gaze drifted to the shadows. “I couldn’t stand by and watch them disappear. They are a warning, child. A reminder of what we’ve lost…and what we’ve been made to forget.” He glanced at the tunnel entrance, then back at Seris. “They suspect. This place is warded, hidden by the very magic they seek to control. But the wards won’t hold forever.” His gaze fell on the Sunstone Bloom sketch poking from her pack. “That flower…it’s a key. A conduit to forgotten memories. They will be coming for it, too.”

Seris met his gaze, her jaw tight. The crimson stain on the sketch felt like a brand, a painful echo of a past she was only beginning to understand. She wasn’t just chasing a rare flower anymore. She was chasing a truth that the Church would kill to keep buried. A cold gust of wind swept through the cavern, extinguishing a nearby oil lamp. In the sudden darkness, Seris knew, with chilling certainty, that Valerius wasn’t just observing them. He was hunting them. And she had a feeling they were running out of time. The weight of forgotten histories, and the dangerous pursuit of remembering, pressed down on Seris like the stone of the ancient hearth.
