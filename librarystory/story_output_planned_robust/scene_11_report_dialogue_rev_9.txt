*   **Repetitive Internal Monologue Leading to Dialogue:** The bulk of this scene is internal thought. While effective for building mystery, it *severely* weakens the impact of the eventual dialogue. The character essentially tells us *everything* before saying anything to Elara. This makes the dialogue feel like a late arrival to a party that's already finished.
*   **"I… I built it.” – Too Direct:** This line, while a crucial reveal, is delivered too bluntly. After pages of internal revelation, it feels flat. Consider *showing* this realization through fragmented speech, a question, or a reaction to something Elara says/does. For example, instead of stating "I built it," perhaps: "It's… familiar. The structure… it's like I *know* where every line of code should be." or even just a stunned, breathy "No…"
*   **“And I remember…” – Weak Pause:** The pause indicated is fine, but the follow-up ("…laying the first stone") feels anticlimactic. It’s a big reveal, but delivered as a statement of fact. Consider making it more evocative or emotional. Perhaps: “…the weight of it… the cold stone… I… I remember the feeling.”
*   **Elara’s Silence – Opportunity Missed:** Elara is a grounding presence, but entirely passive. Her silence is noted, but it’s not *used*. A single line of questioning ("What do you mean?") or a concerned observation ("You're shaking...") would immediately raise the stakes and force the character to articulate their realization instead of simply stating it.
*   **“But the stone wasn’t virtual…” – Telling, Not Showing:** This is another instance of telling the reader information that could be *shown* through reaction or a shift in perception. For example, the character could reach out and instinctively touch a real stone surface, a look of disbelief crossing their face.

**Overall:** The scene relies too heavily on internal monologue and delivers crucial reveals through direct statements rather than dramatic dialogue or action. While the internal experience is well-described, the dialogue feels like an afterthought.
