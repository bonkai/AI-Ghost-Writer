The chill started at the base of my skull, intensifying with each attempt. I reached out again, not with words, but with intent, focusing on the serpent image. It wasn't a clear picture, more a fragment of carved stone, a sinuous curve hinting at ancient power.

This time, contact felt different. Not a blank wall, but a yielding resistance, like pressing against oiled silk. A flicker beyond the stone – a vast, echoing chamber choked with shadow, the faintest impression of watching eyes. Then, a tightening behind my temples, a pressure building with each pulse. I tasted copper, a phantom sensation on my tongue.

“Is everything alright?” Hemlock’s voice, smooth as polished obsidian, cut through my concentration. He hadn’t moved, but his unblinking gaze assessed me, a subtle lift of his eyebrow the only indication of his question.

I forced my eyes open, blinking against the dim light. “Just admiring the architecture,” I stammered, the lie catching in my throat.

I tried again, willing the serpent image to solidify, to *reveal* itself. Energy surged through me, and for a moment, I felt a resonance, not with a being, but with the stone itself, with the Atheneum’s foundations. It felt…old. Immeasurably so. And profoundly unwilling, like forcing a lock with the wrong key. I pushed harder, and a sharp pain lanced through my head.

A bookshelf rattled, a heavy tome tumbling to the floor with a muffled thud. The scent of aged paper and dust filled the air, thick and suffocating. I stumbled back, clutching my temples.

“Careful now,” Hemlock said, a hint of amusement lacing his voice, but his eyes remained cold. He didn’t move to help. “These volumes are fragile. As are your…abilities, it seems.”

I ignored him, pressing through the discomfort, focusing on what the carving *meant*. I sent another probe outwards, a desperate plea. This time, the resistance wasn't yielding silk, but a solid wall of stone. It felt like slamming into a glacier, the impact jarring my teeth. The air grew cold, a subtle stillness descending, muffling all sound. A prickling sensation rose on my skin, as if a storm was brewing *within* the stone.

Then, the wall *moved*. Not physically, but…internally. It wasn’t just blocking me; it was actively repelling me, pushing against my mind with ancient, hostile force. A warning flooded my senses: *Stay away. This is not for you.* The pressure behind my eyes intensified, and I bit down on my tongue, tasting blood.

I recoiled, clutching my head, a groan escaping my lips. The connection severed, leaving a void, a ringing emptiness. I realized I wasn’t communicating with a spirit, but reaching something far older, far more powerful, and it clearly didn’t want to be disturbed. And it had made its displeasure known.

Hemlock stopped a few feet away, dark eyes boring into mine, unreadable. “The Atheneum chooses who it reveals its secrets to,” he murmured, his voice a low caress edged with steel. “And it seems, it has not chosen you. Perhaps it sensed…a hunger exceeding your grasp.” He paused, his gaze sweeping over me with chilling assessment. “Some secrets are best left buried. For your sake, and for those who would be…disappointed by your curiosity.” He took another step closer, his voice dropping to a whisper. “Consider this a warning.”

One last time, I sent a desperate plea outwards, a fragile tendril of intent reaching for the serpent image. 

Nothing. 

I lowered my head, trying to regain control. When I looked up, Hemlock’s gaze met mine. He hadn’t moved, hadn’t offered comfort. Only a silent, knowing assessment. A subtle tightening of his lips – not triumph, but something colder, something…expectant. He was waiting. 

I pressed my fingers to my throbbing temple, a dull ache spreading through my skull. A tremor ran through my hand. He hadn’t just witnessed my failure; he’d anticipated it. And that realization, more than the pain, left me shaken.
