## Draft 3 – Scene 7

“Seris?”

She whirled, heart hammering against her ribs. Hemlock stood framed in the doorway, his expression a carefully constructed mask. He didn’t speak immediately, simply observed, a stillness that felt less composed and more…held. As if bracing for a tremor.

Seris offered a curt nod, her gaze fixed on the sphere. An inexplicable pull tugged at her, a resonance deep within her bones. She extended a hand, fingers hovering just above the cool, smooth surface. A tremor ran through the stone as her fingertips brushed it, a vibration that climbed her arm and settled in her chest. It wasn’t unpleasant, but *awakening* – a stirring of something long dormant within her.

The world didn’t shatter, but *unfolded*. First, a blurring at the edges of her vision, like heat rising off sun-baked stone. Then, layers peeled back, revealing glimpses—not of a place, but of *times*. She saw cities carved into the sides of mountains, their spires crumbling under the weight of ages, then canyons choked with luminous, alien flora. The images weren’t static; they pulsed with a forgotten life, a slow, rhythmic breathing that mirrored her own quickening pulse. Coiled around the highest peak of a basalt mountain, a colossal serpentine creature stirred. Scales shimmered, each one reflecting a thousand sunsets, and the air itself thrummed with its ancient power. Awe, quickly followed by a primal fear, clenched at her throat.

It wasn’t the sheer scale that stole her breath, but the gaze. Amidst the swirling chaos of fractured landscapes, a single, ancient eye met hers—reptilian, the gold flecks within it like trapped starlight. It didn’t merely *see* her; it bored *through* her, stripping away pretense, expectation, even memory. A resonance bloomed in her chest, a hollow ache mirroring the creature’s immeasurable weariness. It felt…familiar. Not a memory, but a recognition – a chord struck within her soul that resonated with something ancient and deeply buried. She stumbled back, a strangled sound escaping her lips, and braced a hand against the wall, knuckles white, breath ragged. The images fractured, receding like a tide pulled back from the shore, leaving a coldness in her chest and a prickling behind her eyes.

*Awakening…* The thought wasn’t spoken, but *felt*—a weightless pressure against her mind, the echo of the serpent’s presence. Not a threat, but an inevitability, steeped in eons and a chilling, patient hunger. It felt… strangely like homecoming. A dizzying wave of disorientation washed over her. She squeezed her eyes shut, then slowly, cautiously opened them.

The chamber seemed heavier, shadows deeper, the thrumming beneath her feet intensified. The sphere radiated heat, the carvings twisting and shifting like living things, as if responding to her touch. A tremor ran through her, not of fear, but of…recognition. A sense of being *known*.

Hemlock’s voice, closer now, broke the silence. “Disturbed?” He had entered the chamber, his gaze sweeping over the sphere, then settling on her face, assessing, searching. A flicker of something unreadable – something akin to *expectation* – crossed his features.

She wrapped her arms around herself, a sudden chill gripping her despite the warmth. “Cities…a serpent…it felt real. Not like a vision, but…a memory. One that isn't mine. It…it felt like looking into my own past, but a past I never lived.”

Hemlock’s lips curved into a faint, unreadable smile. He reached out, fingers hovering over the sphere, then drew back, as if testing the air. “Some places remember. Some objects…hold echoes.” He paused, his gaze locking with hers, searching, *probing*. “And some people…are more attuned.” He didn’t say it as a question, but as a statement of fact.

He studied her intently, his expression unreadable. “Be careful. What sleeps within the stone may not appreciate being disturbed. Or…perhaps it has been *waiting*.” A subtle shift in his gaze suggested he knew far more than he let on, and that he suspected she did too.

She looked back at the sphere, and for a fleeting moment, she thought she saw a flicker of movement within its depths—a subtle shifting of the serpentine patterns, a single, golden eye reflecting her own. The eye didn’t blink. It *watched*. And in its depths, she glimpsed not a reflection of herself, but a flicker of something ancient, something *knowing*. A silent acknowledgement. A promise. Or a warning. And for the first time, a chilling realization dawned: she wasn’t merely *seeing* the past. The past was seeing *her*.
