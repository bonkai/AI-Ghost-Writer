*   **Lucian’s Dialogue – Implied Accusation:** While the intent to be imposing is clear, Lucian's first line ("What have you offered the stone?") feels a little *too* direct, even for an Inquisitor. It lacks subtlety. Consider something that *implies* knowledge rather than demands it. For example: “The stone accepts…offerings?” or “Something’s been left here.” This builds tension through implication, rather than stating the obvious.

*   **Lack of Boy’s Voice:** The entire scene unfolds *around* the boy, but he doesn’t speak. While fear and silence are effective, giving him *something* – even a whimper, a choked “No,” or a desperate plea – would heighten the emotional impact and create a more dynamic exchange. Complete silence makes him feel more like a prop than a character.

*   **Valerius’ Line – Functional, Not Characterful:** “A relic, perhaps?” is purely functional dialogue. It advances the plot but doesn’t reveal anything about Valerius’ personality or his relationship with Lucian. Consider adding a nuance – a hint of skepticism, a professional curiosity, or even a subtle disagreement with Lucian’s approach.

*   **Lucian’s Orders – Cold, but Repetitive:** The instructions to Valerius (“Take him to the Citadel…Retrieve the object…Catalog it…”) are efficient but lack variation. Consider combining some of these into a single, more complex sentence, or adding a small detail that reveals Lucian's authority and precision.

*   **Missed Opportunity for Subtext (Lucian/Boy):** The entire interaction with the boy feels very…transactional. Lucian gets information, the boy is taken away. There’s no sense of Lucian *enjoying* his power, or even being bothered by the boy's fear. A flicker of something – a cold amusement, a weary resignation – would add depth.

*   **Overall – Dialogue Serves Plot, Not Character:** The dialogue primarily *does* things – it delivers information, issues commands, and moves the plot forward. It doesn't often *reveal* character, build relationships, or create emotional resonance. Consider how each line could also contribute to our understanding of the speakers and their motivations.
