*   **Hemlock’s Silence:** While effective to a degree, Hemlock’s extended silence is *almost* too much. It risks feeling less like calculated observation and more like lazy writing. Consider giving him *one* brief, pointed comment earlier in the sequence (before the final exchange) to reinforce his observant, subtly manipulative nature. Something like, after the book falls, "A clumsy approach, wouldn't you say?"
*   **“Admiring the architecture”:** This line feels *very* weak and unbelievable, even for a stammered attempt at deflection. It’s a cliché. Given the character's intelligence and the situation, something more specific, or even a slightly panicked question ("What was that noise?") would be more plausible.
*   **Internal Monologue Disguised as Dialogue:** “It doesn’t want to be found” is almost entirely internal thought presented as spoken dialogue. It doesn’t *need* to be said aloud. If it *must* be voiced, give it a clearer purpose – is she trying to rationalize, or is she hoping Hemlock will offer a counter-argument?
*   **Repetition of Non-Verbal Cues:** The constant reiteration of Hemlock “not moving” and his “cold” gaze is becoming redundant. Show his assessment through *what* he says and *how* he says it, rather than repeatedly *telling* us he's assessing.
*   **Hemlock’s Final Lines – Opportunity for Subtext:** His final speech is good, but could be *sharper*. The “secrets willing to be found” feels a bit on-the-nose. Consider a more oblique warning. Perhaps something that *sounds* like advice, but subtly implies he’s aware of the consequences of her actions. For example: “Some doors, once opened, cannot be easily closed.”
*   **Tremor & Physicality – Show, Don't Tell:** The description of the tremor is fine, but linking it *more directly* to the dialogue would be stronger. Instead of "A tremor ran through my hand," consider having the tremor *affect* her speech – a slight waver in her voice, a difficulty forming words.

Overall, the dialogue isn’t *bad*, but it’s leaning towards telling rather than showing, and could benefit from more nuanced subtext and a reduction in repetitive descriptions.
