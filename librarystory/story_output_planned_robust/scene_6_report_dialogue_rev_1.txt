*   **“Just…admiring the architecture,” I stammered, the lie tasting like ash.** – This is a bit cliché. The “tasting like ash” feels overly dramatic and doesn't add much. Consider a simpler, more internal reaction to the lie – a quickening pulse, a feeling of heat in the cheeks.
*   **“Careful now,” Hemlock said, his voice laced with a dangerous amusement. He didn't move to help. “These volumes are quite fragile. And so are your…abilities, it seems.”** – While effective, this feels a little *too* on the nose. Hemlock is clearly being condescending and subtly threatening. Consider removing the "And so are your…abilities, it seems" line entirely. The condescension is already clear from his inaction and tone. Less is often more.
*   **“I’m fine,” I said, my voice tight with effort. “I just…felt a bit lightheaded.”** – Again, a bit cliché. The “tight with effort” is telling us what to feel, not *showing* it. A shorter, more fragmented response would be more effective. Perhaps just, “Fine. Just… dizzy.”
*   **Hemlock’s longer speeches (especially the final one) are a bit expository.** He's essentially *telling* us his motivations and the stakes, rather than letting them emerge through subtext and action. Consider trimming these down and letting his *delivery* (described in the narrative) do more of the work. For example, instead of "Some secrets are best left buried...", perhaps just, "The Atheneum doesn’t share easily."
*   **The dialogue is generally quite direct.** While clarity isn't an issue, there's a lack of subtext. The characters say what they mean, rather than hinting at deeper motivations or feelings. This makes the scene feel a little flat. Consider adding layers of meaning to the dialogue, letting the characters say one thing while implying another.
*   **Repetition of internal state descriptions followed by dialogue.** The pattern of "I felt X, then I said Y" is occurring frequently. This slows the pacing and feels redundant. Try to weave the internal experience *into* the dialogue, or show it through action.



Overall, the dialogue is *functional* but lacks nuance and depth. It's not *bad*, but it could be significantly improved with more subtext, tighter phrasing, and a greater reliance on showing rather than telling.
