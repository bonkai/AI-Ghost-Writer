*   **Repetitive phrasing:** The constant reiteration of "he's..." and "they're..." (e.g., "He's testing," "He's letting," "They're the pieces") feels clunky and lacks subtlety. Consider varying sentence structure and using more implied meaning. Instead of *telling* us Marius is playing a game, *show* it through his actions and Seris's reactions.
*   **Seris's internal state - show, don't tell:** Seris frequently *states* her feelings ("I know," "I feel," "It felt wrong"). While internal monologue is fine, much of this could be conveyed through her body language, reactions, and choices. For example, instead of "It felt wrong, condescending," describe her physical reaction to the man’s gaze – a tightening of her jaw, a prickling sensation on her skin, etc.
*   **Kaelen’s Unreadable Expressions:** Kaelen's "unreadable" expressions are mentioned multiple times. This becomes a crutch. While mystery is good, consistently *telling* us his expression is unreadable doesn’t create tension; it avoids revealing his thoughts and feelings. Consider *showing* his internal state through subtle actions or brief, revealing dialogue.
*   **Exposition via Dialogue:** The explanation of the Circle and the scholars (“They believed Firepeak held the key,” “The scholars here sought to understand…”) feels a bit forced. While necessary information, it’s delivered as direct exposition. Could some of this be revealed through environmental details or Seris’s observations?
*   **Subtext Opportunity:** The pity in the man's eyes is a strong image. Explore this more. Why pity? What does he know that Seris doesn't? This could be a rich source of subtext and tension. Instead of Seris simply *feeling* condescended to, have her react in a way that reveals her pride or defiance.
*   **Kaelen’s final line:** “And he’s likely already prepared for us to defy them” feels a bit too on-the-nose and predictable. It removes the tension. Consider ending on a more ambiguous note, leaving the reader (and Seris) wondering what Marius expects.

Overall, the dialogue isn’t *bad*, but it relies heavily on telling rather than showing and could benefit from more subtlety, subtext, and indirect characterization. The characters frequently state the obvious instead of letting their actions and reactions speak for themselves.
