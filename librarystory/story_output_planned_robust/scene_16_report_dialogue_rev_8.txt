*   **“Seris?” / “What do you see?”** – These feel like very direct questions in a moment of intense, internal experience for Seris. While functional, they lack subtlety. Consider if Rhys could *show* concern through action (tightening his grip further, a change in his posture) *before* asking a direct question, or if the question could be rephrased to reflect his observation of her distress ("Seris, you shook. What happened?").
*   **“It’s connected to the source of the network,” / “Something is contained within it, ancient and terrible. The seal is failing, and the cracks are widest there.”** – This is a large amount of exposition delivered very quickly. While the urgency is understandable, it feels like Seris is *telling* Rhys information rather than *revealing* it through a more fragmented, emotional explanation. Could some of this be implied through her physical reaction or a more evocative description of the vision? For example, instead of "The seal is failing," perhaps "It's…breaking. I can *feel* it unraveling."
*   **“We have to go. Before whatever is contained within breaks free.”** – Again, very direct. The urgency is good, but the line lacks nuance. Consider a more desperate plea, or a statement that focuses on her *feeling* the danger rather than stating it as fact. ("We *have* to go. Now. I don't know how long it will hold.")
*   **Voice & Given Information:** Seris's dialogue frequently feels like she’s relaying information she *knows* rather than processing a chaotic vision. The phrase “the words feeling…given, not reasoned” is excellent internal description, but the dialogue itself doesn’t *reflect* that feeling. It reads as analytical, not overwhelmed.
*   **Rhys's Lack of Dialogue:** Rhys is almost entirely reactive. While a stoic observer is a valid character choice, consider giving him *some* dialogue that reveals his internal state or challenges Seris’s conclusions (even briefly). This would create more dynamic interaction.



Overall, the dialogue isn't *bad*, but it leans heavily towards functional information delivery. Injecting more subtext, emotional vulnerability, and indirect characterization would elevate the scene.
