Dust motes danced in the lamplight, a familiar comfort that did little to ease the prickle across Seris’s skin. She deepened the shading on the Sunstone Bloom’s petals, the nib scratching a delicate crimson stain onto the vellum. The color felt less a reflection of the flower, and more a mirroring of the pressure building within her.

Rhys traced a finger across a faded illustration in a crumbling tome – a precarious rock face on Firepeak. He didn’t meet her eyes, but his grip tightened on the page, the parchment crinkling almost imperceptibly. “Landslides are common after rainfall,” he said, his voice clipped, as if reciting a memorized warning.

A shadow fell across their worktable, not abrupt, but a gradual obscuring of the lamplight. Valerius didn’t approach directly, but lingered, observing their workspace – the scattered texts, the half-finished sketch, the curated chaos. The air seemed to thicken, each breath suddenly deliberate.

“Still chasing whispers?” Valerius asked, his voice smooth, carrying a subtle weight. He didn’t ask it as a question, but a statement, an accusation barely veiled.

Rhys offered Seris’s sketch with a slight tilt of his hand – less a presentation, more a deflection, a shield raised against scrutiny.

Valerius’s gaze followed, landing on the drawing of the Sunstone Bloom. He studied it for a long moment, his expression unreadable. A flicker – curiosity, perhaps, or something colder – crossed his face, then vanished before Seris could decipher it. He didn’t comment on the sketch itself.

“The Sunstone Bloom,” he said slowly. “A rare specimen. Legends say it blooms only under the light of the crimson moon – a phenomenon rarely witnessed.” The statement hung in the air, implying a knowledge beyond simple botany.

Seris met Valerius’s gaze, her expression carefully neutral, but a pulse throbbed at her temple, betraying her composure. “We’ve found accounts of its use in ancient healing practices, among the mountain tribes. We’re hoping to corroborate those claims.” She kept her voice even, refusing to offer more than necessary.

“Firepeak Mountain is dangerous. Many have ventured into those peaks and never returned.” The scent of his sandalwood cologne drifted towards them, cloying and unsettling, a deliberate assertion of his presence.

Rhys’s fingers tightened on the tome’s page, the parchment crinkling. “We understand the risks. We’ve been meticulously researching the terrain, the weather patterns…” He avoided Valerius’s eyes, focusing intently on the faded illustrations, as if the answers were hidden within the aged paper.

Valerius’s gaze swept over them, assessing, measuring. The silence stretched, punctuated only by the crackle of the lamplight. Then, a slow smile touched his lips, but didn't reach his eyes.

“Perhaps you are prepared.” He paused, letting the ambiguity linger. “Though the mountain…tests preparations.” His eyes locked with Seris’s, holding her gaze for a beat too long, a silent challenge.

Seris held his gaze, her expression unwavering, but a tremor ran through her fingers, barely contained, betraying the fear she refused to acknowledge.

Valerius’s smile widened, unsettlingly cold. “I look forward to hearing of your discoveries…or misfortunes.” He turned, his cloak swirling around him, and moved away, leaving Seris and Rhys bathed in the flickering lamplight.

Rhys’s hand briefly covered hers, a fleeting pressure – a silent question, a shared apprehension – before he returned to the text, his knuckles white. The crimson stain on the vellum seemed to pulse, a dark bloom against the pale parchment. A coldness settled over Seris, a certainty that Valerius hadn’t believed a word they’d said, and that their journey had become far more dangerous than they’d anticipated.
