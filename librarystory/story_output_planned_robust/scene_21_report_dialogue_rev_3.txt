*   **Rhys's initial line ("Healing? Curious...") feels a bit *too* practiced and defensive.** It immediately signals something is wrong, making the subtlety of Marius’s probing less effective. Consider a more straightforward, less immediately reactive response. Perhaps simply acknowledging the documentation without the added defensiveness.
*   **Seris's "The pursuit of knowledge is never commonplace..." is a bit cliché and feels like a direct retort rather than a natural response.** It's a noble sentiment, but doesn't feel organically *her* in the moment. A more nuanced response acknowledging the Inquisitor's point but subtly defending her work would be stronger.
*   **Kaelen's telepathic line ("He’s afraid...") is a bit of an info-dump.** While useful for the reader, it feels unnatural for a telepath to simply *state* the obvious to Seris. Consider showing Seris *feeling* his fear, or Kaelen offering a more cryptic observation that leads Seris to the same conclusion.
*   **Seris's repeated assertions of honesty ("We have no secrets," "Everyone has secrets...") are becoming repetitive and feel like she's protesting too much.** While understandable given the situation, it's losing impact. Consider replacing one instance with a more subtle action or a pointed question.
*   **Marius's final line ("Everyone has secrets…") is a bit of a standard villain trope.** It's not *bad*, but feels predictable. Consider a more unique threat or a final observation that hints at his specific motivations.
*   **The dialogue relies heavily on stating intent/feeling ("He’s afraid," "She wouldn’t be intimidated," "projecting an unwavering confidence she barely felt").** Show, don't tell. Let the characters' actions and body language convey these things. For example, instead of "projecting an unwavering confidence she barely felt," describe her tightening grip on the pouch, a slight tremor in her voice, or a forced smile.
*   **While the tension is good, the dialogue is a little *too* direct.** Marius is essentially announcing his suspicions. More subtlety, more veiled threats, and more opportunities for misinterpretation would create a more compelling dynamic.
