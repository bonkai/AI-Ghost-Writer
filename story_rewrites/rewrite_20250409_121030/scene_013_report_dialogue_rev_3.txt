*   **Repetitive phrasing:** Both Leo and Anya use "It's hard to explain, sir" / "I can't explain, sir." It's a natural response, but happening in quick succession feels weak. Consider varying the phrasing – “It’s…difficult to articulate, sir,” or having them *try* to explain, even if failing, to show their thought process.
*   **Volkov's Dialogue:** Volkov's responses are *extremely* clipped. While this establishes his personality, it borders on robotic. A little more variation in tone, even subtle, would make him feel more like a person and less like a command prompt. Consider adding a brief acknowledgement of their concern, or a frustrated sigh.
*   **"They're waking up" - Subtext opportunity:** GhostSignal's reply is a bit on the nose. While effective, it could be more impactful if it were more cryptic. Something like, “The resonance is increasing,” or “The threshold is breached.” Let the reader connect the dots more fully.
*   **Leo’s hesitation:** The line “Leo hesitated” *before* saying “It feels…conscious, sir” is telling, but a stronger *showing* of that hesitation within the dialogue itself would be more effective. Maybe a stutter, a trailing sentence, or a self-correction.
*   **Anya’s “Containment breach”:** This feels a little too conveniently revealed. While it sets up the plot, the delivery is a bit flat. Consider having her struggle to decipher the transmission, or only getting fragments of the message before piecing it together. The tension would increase.
*   **Matching Code - Dialogue Opportunity:** The fact that Leo and Anya *immediately* realize the codes match feels fast. A beat of confusion, of them double-checking their data, would add realism and build suspense. A line like, “Wait…that’s the same sequence,” or “That can’t be a coincidence,” would be effective.
*   **Overall:** The dialogue is functional and conveys information, but it leans towards directness. More opportunities for subtext, internal conflict shown *through* dialogue, and subtle characterization would elevate it.
