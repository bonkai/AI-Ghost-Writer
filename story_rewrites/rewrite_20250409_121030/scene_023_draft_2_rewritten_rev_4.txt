The valley breathed a fragile peace. Dust motes danced in the late afternoon sun, illuminating the patchwork fields of nutrient crops. Anya traced the rim of her mug, the recycled ceramic warm against her fingers. It wasn’t the rich, dark clay of Old Earth, but it held heat. She missed the weight of it, the feel of something *real* in her hands. A tremor ran through the floor, almost imperceptible, and she paused, listening.

“Signal’s stronger,” Tiber grumbled from the comm station, his voice rough as shale. “Definitely not atmospheric. Origin… sector Gamma-Nine. Deep within the Blasted Lands.”

Anya rose, her boots silent on the packed earth floor. The others – Kael, Elara, and Bram – were already gathered around the holographic display, the jagged peaks of Gamma-Nine glowing a sickly purple.

“Blasted Lands?” Kael’s brow furrowed. “That sector’s been dead for decades. Nothing but radiation and dust storms.”

“Exactly,” Tiber said, his gaze fixed on the readings. “Which makes this… unusual.”

The signal pulsed on the display, a faint, erratic heartbeat against the purple static. It wasn’t a broadcast. It wasn’t a beacon. It was… something else. A low hum resonated through the floor, barely audible, but Anya felt it in her teeth.

“Could be a malfunction,” Elara suggested, her voice tight. “A ghost echo from the old war?”

“I’ve filtered for that,” Tiber countered. “This is new. And it’s growing stronger.”

Anya stepped closer, her hand hovering over the map. “Sector Gamma-Nine… that’s where the SimuLife facility was located.”

A hush fell over the group. SimuLife. The name tasted like ash in Anya’s mouth. The pre-Collapse corporation that had promised to solve the food crisis, only to unleash a plague upon the land.

“We can take precautions,” Anya said, her voice firm. “Protective suits, radiation shielding. We go in, scan the area, and come back. A quick reconnaissance mission.”

Kael studied the holographic display, his skepticism slowly giving way to curiosity. “What kind of technology did SimuLife have? Could this signal be some kind of automated system, still functioning after all these years?”

“Probably a trap,” Elara insisted. She ran a hand through her hair, her knuckles white. “My mother spent years cleaning up their mess. It’s poisoned ground, Anya. Best left undisturbed.”

Anya met Elara’s gaze, sensing a deeper fear beneath her resistance. Elara’s mother had been a botanist, one of the few who’d worked tirelessly to contain the SimuLife blight. Anya remembered stories of her mother’s heartbreak, her desperate attempts to undo the damage, the exhaustion that ultimately claimed her.

“We can take precautions,” Anya repeated, her voice softer this time. “We go in prepared. It’s a risk, yes, but ignoring it could be a bigger one.”

Kael nodded, a flicker of excitement in his eyes. “Alright. I’m in. I want to see what they were working on.”

Bram shifted his weight, his expression reluctant. “I still think it’s a fool’s errand,” he said, “but if you’re going, someone needs to pilot the scout vehicle. I’ll go.” He paused, then added, “I doubted it at first, but I see the necessity.”

Anya turned to Elara, hoping for some sign of acceptance. Elara remained silent, her face pale. A tremor ran through the floor again, stronger this time, and she flinched. “Just… be careful,” she finally said, her voice strained. “And don’t stay long.”

Anya nodded. “We will.”

---

The scout vehicle hummed with power as it navigated the desolate landscape of Gamma-Nine. Dust devils danced across the cracked earth, obscuring the skeletal remains of the SimuLife facility. Anya scanned the readings, her heart pounding in her chest. The air tasted metallic, and a low, rhythmic thrum vibrated through the vehicle's hull.

“Signal’s getting stronger,” she reported over the comm. “It’s originating from the central research complex.”

As they approached the complex, a chilling silence descended. The air crackled with static. Anya felt a prickling sensation on the back of her neck. The thrumming grew louder, resonating in her chest.

Then, on the radar screen, a new reading appeared. A faint, irregular pulse. It wasn’t a clean signal, but a chaotic burst of energy, almost… organic.

Anya didn’t mention it, but a knot of unease tightened in her chest. The pulse on the radar flickered again, a short, erratic burst, and then… silence. It felt less like a signal, and more like a heartbeat. A slow, deliberate heartbeat, echoing from the ruins of a forgotten past. The vehicle shuddered, and Anya glanced at Kael, who was staring intently at his sensors.

“Did you feel that?” she asked.

Kael didn’t answer, his eyes fixed on the readings. “It’s… fluctuating,” he said, his voice barely a whisper. “The energy signature… it’s changing.”
