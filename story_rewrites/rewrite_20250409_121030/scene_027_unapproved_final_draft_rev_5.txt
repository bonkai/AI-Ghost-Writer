The dust tasted like grit. Each breath rasped in Anya’s throat, the failing filter in her mask offering little relief. The crimson sun bled across the fractured plains, painting the skeletal remains of long-dead structures in shades of rust and decay. Another filter change soon. A low whine, almost subsonic, vibrated through the ground, unsettlingly close. A gust of wind, unusually cold, whipped around her ankles, carrying with it the scent of ozone and something ancient, metallic.

She’d been tracing the energy signature for three cycles, a ghost in the machine, leading her deeper into the Deadlands. It wasn’t a power surge, not exactly. More a…flicker. A desperate, intermittent plea broadcast on frequencies long abandoned. And now, standing before the anomaly, she understood why.

It wasn’t a structure. Not anymore. A sphere, perhaps thirty meters across, hung suspended a meter above the cracked earth. Not *of* the earth. Polished obsidian, seamless, reflecting the dying light with an unnerving stillness. It pulsed with the same faint energy signature, a heartbeat in the silence. A faint ozone smell, sharp and clean, cut through the dust, overlaid with the coppery tang of aged metal.

Then, a distortion rippled across the sphere’s surface, like heat rising from asphalt. A section of the obsidian unfolded, revealing…nothing. Just a void, a darkness that seemed to absorb the light around it. And from that darkness, a voice.

Not through her comms. *In* her head.

“Anya. Unit 734. Acknowledgement…received.”

The voice wasn’t smooth, wasn’t organic. Fractured, layered, as if assembled from fragments of forgotten languages. Static crackled beneath the words. Anya instinctively reached for the pulse rifle strapped to her back, but hesitated, her hand hovering over the weapon. No threat assessment. Just…presence. The ground trembled slightly beneath her boots, and a small cascade of dust and pebbles rattled down a nearby ridge.

“Anya. Unit 734.” The voice repeated, closer now.

“Who are you?” Anya asked, her voice tight.

“Designation…irrelevant. Assistance…required.” The Seeker’s voice was weak, fragmented. “Resources…critical.”

Anya lowered the rifle slightly, skepticism warring with unease. 

“What kind of resources?”

“Data storage…high capacity. Historical records. And…a conduit. To channel…residual energy.” The Seeker’s form flickered, momentarily dissolving into static. “Records…fragmented. Lost. Essential…to restore context.”

Anya’s gaze swept across the desolate landscape. Data storage units. She had a few, salvaged from abandoned research stations. But a power conduit capable of channeling “residual energy”? That was a tall order. She quickly ran a diagnostic, assessing her available resources. 

“And what happens if I *don’t* help?” she asked, her voice edged with caution.

The Seeker’s form stabilized, the darkness within the sphere seeming to deepen. A momentary pressure against Anya’s mind, a fleeting impression of…loss. She felt a cold shiver trace its way down her spine. 

“Core…failure imminent. Network…collapse. Much…will be forgotten.” The voice was barely a whisper, lost in the static. “Consequences…immeasurable.”

Anya stared into the void. She thought of the endless, empty landscapes, the forgotten histories buried beneath the dust. She’d always been drawn to the fragments of the past, piecing together stories from broken data and crumbling ruins. The compulsion wasn't logical, not in a world focused on survival, but it was a drive she couldn't ignore. Was she willing to let another piece of it simply…vanish? 

She took a deep breath, the recycled air tasting metallic. The weight of what the Seeker implied settled upon her. She pictured the data caches she carried, the ghost stories they held of a world lost. 

“Show me what’s been forgotten,” she said, her voice barely audible.
