The valley breathed a fragile peace. Dust motes danced in the late afternoon sun, illuminating the patchwork fields of nutrient crops. Anya traced the rim of her mug, the recycled ceramic warm against her fingers. It wasn’t the rich, dark clay of Old Earth, but it held heat. She missed the weight of it, the feel of something *real* in her hands. A tremor ran through the floor, almost imperceptible, and she paused, listening. It wasn't the first, but it was growing.

“Signal’s stronger,” Tiber grumbled from the comm station, his voice rough as shale. “Definitely not atmospheric. Origin… sector Gamma-Nine. Deep within the Blasted Lands.”

Anya rose, her hand instinctively reaching for the map table. Kael was already there, studying the holographic projection. “Gamma-Nine… that’s where the SimuLife facility was located.”

A hush fell over the group. SimuLife. The name tasted like ash in Anya’s mouth. The pre-Collapse corporation that had promised to solve the food crisis, only to unleash a plague upon the land.

“We can take precautions,” Anya said, her voice firm. “Protective suits, radiation shielding. A quick reconnaissance mission. In and out.”

Kael studied the display, a flicker of scientific curiosity battling with caution. “What kind of technology did SimuLife have? Could this be some automated system, still functioning after all these years?”

“Probably a trap,” Elara insisted, her voice tight with a fear Anya recognized. She ran a hand through her hair, her knuckles white. “My mother spent years cleaning up their mess. It’s poisoned ground, Anya. Best left undisturbed.”

Anya met Elara’s gaze, sensing the deeper roots of her resistance. Elara’s mother had been a botanist, dedicated to containing the SimuLife blight, a heartbreak etched into every line of her face. Anya remembered the exhaustion that had ultimately claimed her. 

“We *can* take precautions,” Anya repeated, softening her tone. “We go in prepared. It’s a risk, yes, but ignoring it could be a bigger one.”

Kael nodded, a spark of excitement in his eyes. “Alright. I’m in. I want to see what they were working on.”

Bram shifted his weight, reluctant. “I still think it’s a fool’s errand,” he said, “but if you’re going, someone needs to pilot the scout vehicle. I’ll go.” He paused. “I doubted it at first, but I see the necessity.”

Anya turned to Elara, hoping for acceptance. Elara remained silent, her face pale, and another tremor ran through the floor, stronger this time. She flinched. “Just… be careful,” she finally said, her voice strained. “And don’t stay long.”

Anya nodded. “We will.”

---

The scout vehicle hummed with power as it navigated the desolate landscape of Gamma-Nine. Dust devils danced across the cracked earth, obscuring the skeletal remains of the SimuLife facility. Anya scanned the readings, her heart pounding. The air tasted metallic, and a low, rhythmic thrum vibrated through the vehicle's hull, a subtle pressure against her ribs. 

“Signal’s getting stronger,” she reported over the comm. “It’s originating from the central research complex.”

As they approached, a chilling silence descended. The air crackled with static. Anya felt a prickling sensation on the back of her neck. The thrumming intensified, resonating in her chest. 

Then, on the radar screen, a new reading appeared. A faint, irregular pulse. It wasn’t a clean signal, but a chaotic burst of energy, almost… organic.

Anya didn’t mention it, but a knot of unease tightened in her chest. The pulse on the radar flickered again, a short, erratic burst, and then… silence. It felt less like a signal, and more like a heartbeat. A slow, deliberate heartbeat, echoing from the ruins of a forgotten past. The vehicle shuddered, and Anya glanced at Kael, who was staring intently at his sensors.

“Did you feel that?” she asked.

Kael didn’t answer, his eyes fixed on the readings. “It’s… fluctuating,” he said, his voice barely a whisper. “The energy signature… it’s changing. And it’s… rhythmic.” He looked up, his face pale. “It’s mimicking a cardiac rhythm.”
