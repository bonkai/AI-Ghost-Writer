The wind tasted of rust and regret. Anya traced the crumbling concrete of the observation post, a relic of a forgotten age. Below, the valley was a patchwork of withered fields and skeletal trees. It hadn’t rained properly in years. Her community, the Keepers, eked out a living from the stubborn earth, haunted by whispers of what *had* been.

Old Man Tiber adjusted the salvaged binoculars. “Nothing but dust devils today, child. Same as yesterday.”

Anya didn’t bother to look. She knew the view by heart. The Keepers scavenged, repaired, *remembered*. They clung to the stories of the Before Times, a world of shimmering towers and effortless abundance, a world their ancestors had deliberately left behind. A world she couldn't quite grasp, but felt compelled to understand.

“They say the cities… just *vanished*,” she murmured, her gaze fixed on the desolate landscape.

He grunted. “Vanished into a choice. A gilded cage, they called it. Perfect, but a cage nonetheless.”

Anya felt a familiar ache. The stories didn't explain *why* they’d chosen the cage. What had been so terrible about the real world? What had driven them to abandon it? She pressed a hand against the cool concrete, a silent question echoing in the emptiness.

Suddenly, a flicker on the ancient radar screen, a ghost of a signal. Tiber straightened, his weathered hand hovering over the controls.

“What is that?” Anya asked, her voice tight.

“Impossible,” Tiber muttered, adjusting the frequency. “Localized energy surge. Sector Gamma. Not natural.”

The signal strengthened, a pulsing green blip, then vanished, leaving only static. Tiber frowned, studying the readings.

“These weren’t disruptions,” Anya said, pointing to a faded schematic. “Precursors. Happened before the West Watch disappeared.”

“Coincidence,” Kael offered, entering the post. “Storms disrupt everything.”

“These sectors… directly above the old SimuLife facility. The elders warned us.” Anya highlighted readings on the screen. “Matching the theoretical models for SimuLife’s core.”

“SimuLife was a myth,” Elara said, entering the post, her voice firm. “A cautionary tale.”

“The data suggests otherwise.” Anya displayed the readings. “Something is *active* down there.”

“Active?” Kael scoffed. “After all these years?”

“I propose a reconnaissance mission,” Anya declared. “A small team to investigate Sector Gamma. We need to know what’s causing these surges.”

“It’s too dangerous,” Elara protested. “Unstable sector, sandstorms… disturbing something dormant could have unforeseen consequences.”

“We can prepare. Reinforced shelters, filters… we’ve faced worse.” Anya met Elara’s gaze. “If SimuLife is reactivating, it could threaten the entire valley.”

“And if it’s just a natural phenomenon?” Bram asked, entering the post, his voice pragmatic. “We risk lives and resources.”

“A risk I’m willing to take,” Anya replied. “But I won’t ask anyone to go who doesn’t believe it’s worth investigating.”

A tense silence filled the post. Bram shook his head. “Foolish. But… if you’re going, I’ll go with you. Someone needs to keep you alive.”

“I’ll go too,” Kael said, running a hand over his chin. “I want to see this SimuLife for myself.”

Elara sighed, her face etched with concern. “Very well. But we proceed with caution. And honor the elders’ warnings.”

“Good,” Anya replied. “Bram, logistics. Kael, environmental shielding. Elara, research SimuLife’s failsafe protocols. We leave at dawn.” She paused, a chilling certainty settling upon her. Ignoring the signal could be a far greater risk. 

As the others dispersed, Elara lingered, her gaze fixed on the radar screen. A faint, almost imperceptible pulse flickered across the display, then vanished. She didn’t mention it, but a knot of unease tightened in her chest. Something about this felt… different.
