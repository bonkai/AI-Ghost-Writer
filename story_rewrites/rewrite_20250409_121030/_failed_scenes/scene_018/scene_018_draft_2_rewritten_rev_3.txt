The wind tasted of rust and regret. Anya traced the chipped ceramic of a salvaged datapad, its screen crazed like frozen water. Around her, the skeletal remains of skyscrapers clawed at the bruised twilight sky. This was the Scavenger’s Reach, and she wasn’t hunting salvage today. She was listening. For weeks, a subtle static had bled into the long-range comms, a rhythmic pulse beneath the noise. Old Man Tiber dismissed it as solar flares. Anya wasn’t so sure. It felt…intentional. She adjusted the gain on her headset, the whine sharpening, then subtly shifting. Not random. A tremor ran through her hand, and she clenched her fist, trying to steady it.

Kael approached, his boots crunching on the sand, his hand instinctively checking the charge of the energy pistol at his hip. He didn’t bother with a greeting. “Anything?”

Anya shook her head, but angled the datapad’s speaker towards him. A faint, structured hum overlaid the static. “It’s stronger. Structured. Originating from sector four, near the old refinery.”

Kael scanned the horizon, his gaze lingering on the dust devils dancing between the ruined towers. He spat a stream of dust onto the sand. “Refinery’s a graveyard. Scavengers, ghosts, and nothing else. Unless you count Tiber’s stories.” He adjusted the worn leather strap of his pistol holster, a habitual gesture.

“I’m going to check it out.” Anya’s jaw tightened, and she rubbed her forearm, a nervous habit.

---

Leo traced the signal’s origin on the holographic display, the image resolving into a landscape of ruins. He’d bypassed seven security protocols, fabricating a ‘historical analysis’ report to justify the bandwidth allocation. The woman, silhouetted against the dying sun, held a salvaged datapad, her head tilted, *listening*. He zoomed in, noting the set of her jaw, the lines around her eyes. He cross-referenced her biometric signature with the SimuLife database. No match. Impossible. The system insisted all inhabitants were accounted for, their lives meticulously charted. 

He activated the atmospheric scanner, analyzing the composition of the air in her location. Nitrogen, oxygen, trace amounts of methane…a breathable atmosphere. But the readings fluctuated, spiking and dropping erratically. A glitch in the system? Or something…else?

---

The sphere pulsed with a faint internal glow, radiating a low hum that vibrated through the sand, making the fillings in Anya’s teeth ache. Kael circled it cautiously, his pistol raised, his knuckles white. He kicked at the sand around its base. “What is that thing? Some kind of signal buoy?”

Anya reached out, her fingers brushing against the cool metal. The sphere activated, projecting a fragmented hologram. A face flickered in and out of static.

Old Man Tiber began chanting louder, his eyes wide with terror. “A spirit! A machine-god!”

Anya ignored him, focusing on the desperate plea in the fragmented voice. “What system?”

The hologram flickered again, the face dissolving into static, then reforming, barely visible. “The Foundation. The…Everything.” Then, silence. The sphere went dark, the hum ceasing abruptly. 

Anya exchanged a look with Kael. He’d lowered his weapon slightly, his brow furrowed. “We need to keep this quiet. This is bigger than anything we’ve ever seen.” Kael ran a hand through his thinning hair. “This doesn’t feel right. Feels…old.”

---

Leo had established a continuous observational link, dedicating all available resources to the woman and her surroundings. He’d overridden the SimuLife network’s core security protocols, risking complete system failure. He didn’t care. He *had* to understand.

He discovered traces of advanced technology beneath the layers of decay – energy conduits, data storage units, remnants of a civilization far beyond anything he’d ever encountered. This wasn’t just a lost world. It was a lost *future*. A future that, according to all established timelines, should never have existed. He focused the audio feed on the sphere, filtering out the static. He could hear Anya’s voice, questioning, probing. He couldn’t understand the language, but he sensed her determination, her growing understanding. He felt a strange connection, a resonance across the vast gulf of time and space.

He knew he was on the verge of something extraordinary. Something dangerous. Something that could shatter the foundations of his reality. And, with a growing sense of dread, he realized he wasn’t just observing a collapse. He was *witnessing* one. The system was failing, and this lost world, this impossible future, was the cause. And he was running out of time to understand why.
