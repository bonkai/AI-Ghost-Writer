*   **Kael’s Dialogue – Redundancy & Showing, Not Telling:** Kael asks “What’s happening?” and “What does it *mean*?” both very close together. While understandable given the situation, it feels repetitive. Consider merging these into one question, or letting his *actions* (like testing the baton charge) speak for his concern/demand for understanding. The action is stronger.
*   **Anya’s Dialogue – Passive Observation:** Anya largely *reacts* with short phrases (“I… don’t know,” “They’re worried”). While this establishes her as more analytical, it makes her feel a little flat. Consider giving her one line of *interpretation* earlier in the sequence – a slightly more developed thought about what the images *might* be, even if it’s wrong. This establishes her as an active participant in understanding, not just a witness.
*   **Machine’s Monotone – Opportunity for Subtext:** The machine's monotone is effective for establishing its non-human nature. However, consider *briefly* varying the delivery of "Contingency: Simulation." Even a slight pause or emphasis could subtly hint at the weight of that statement.
*   **“They’re worried” – Weak Line:** This line feels like telling, not showing. Anya *sees* worried faces. Can you convey that worry through her description of their expressions instead of stating it directly? (e.g., “Their faces were tight with concern, eyes darting between the readings and each other.”)
*   **Overall – Dialogue is Functional, Not Dynamic:** The dialogue serves the plot, but it doesn't feel particularly *characterful* or emotionally resonant. It’s efficient, but leans towards being a delivery system for information. Consider adding small touches of personality – a habitual phrase for Kael, a more thoughtful pause for Anya – to make the conversation feel more alive.
