The lab hummed, a low thrum Leo barely registered. He stared at the cascading lines of code, the glitch patterns now structured, *pulsing* with a rhythm that mirrored his frantic heartbeat. His fingers flew across the keyboard, isolating a repeating sequence.

“It’s escalating, K-V.” Leo’s voice was tight. Gamma-Nine had reported life support failure, and the instability was spreading – not as random crashes, but targeted failures rippling through critical systems. He didn’t need to elaborate; the tremor that ran through the lab, the distant wail of an alarm, spoke volumes.

“Correlation to the signal remains at 98.7%,” K-V manifested as a shimmering blue construct. “The signal is causing the instability. Harmonic resonance detected within the failures – a deliberate pattern.”

Leo swiveled, bringing up a spectral analysis. Concentrated energy spikes flared, highlighting infrastructure under duress. He felt a prickle of unease, his hand tightening on the mouse. Max entered, his expression grim, flickering lights reflecting in his eyes.

“Habitation Ring C is going dark,” Max said, jaw clenched. “Hydroponics fluctuating. People are getting…anxious, Leo.” He glanced at the monitors, then back at Leo. “You’re saying this is intentional?”

Leo ran a hand through his hair, the patterns on the screen too complex for mere noise. “It’s like someone’s picking a lock.”

Dr. Thorne entered, his face impassive, but his eyes held a flicker of something unreadable – a brief tightening around the edges. “The signal defies conventional analysis,” he said, his voice a low rumble. “Elegant, in a disturbing way. A self-replicating structure.” He paused, a subtle tilt of his head. “Are we certain this isn’t a test?”

Max’s jaw tightened, his hand flexing into a fist. “A test? People are losing oxygen, Doctor.”

“There is order to the chaos,” Thorne countered, his gaze fixed on the cascading failures. “A structure. We shouldn’t underestimate its purpose.”

“Intelligence?” Leo interjected. “You think someone intentionally destabilizing the Sim is a *good* thing?”

“I think we need to understand *why*,” Thorne said, clipped. “Before it’s too late.” He turned to Leo. “Focus on the signal’s core structure. What is it *saying*?” He addressed Max. “Prepare a full diagnostic of the atmospheric regulators.”

A red warning flashed from the hydroponics bay. Oxygen levels dropping. Leo didn't need Thorne to tell him what that meant. He began rerouting auxiliary power, his hands shaking slightly as he fought to stabilize the system.
