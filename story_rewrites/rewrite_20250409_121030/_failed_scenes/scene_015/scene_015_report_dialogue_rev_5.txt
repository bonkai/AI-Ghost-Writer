*   **Old Man Tiber’s Dialogue:** While evocative, Tiber’s initial lines (“We thought it was an escape… it was an escape from what caused it”) feel a bit *too* explanatory. It’s a classic info-dump disguised as cryptic wisdom. Consider breaking this up – perhaps just the initial "The Shift..." followed by a beat of silence, letting Anya or Leo *ask* what it was, prompting Tiber to reveal information more organically.
*   **Leo’s Single Line:** Leo only has one line ("How can a city… be inside?"). He’s present for a massive reveal, and his reaction is… minimal. This feels like a missed opportunity. Even a short, stunned reaction – a gasp, a disbelieving shake of the head – would add depth.
*   **Anya’s Internal Monologue:** Much of Anya’s realization is delivered through internal monologue ("It’s not just damaged…"). While understandable given the urgency, it limits interaction and character development. Could some of this be externalized as quick questions or observations directed at Leo, even if he doesn’t have an immediate answer? (“Leo, the comms are dead. Completely dead.”)
*   **The Voice – Too Much Telling:** The voice announcing “Containment breach detected…” and “The Architects are returning…” is very direct. Consider *showing* the failing systems through sound design or visual cues *before* the voice explains it. Let the audience experience the instability, then have the voice act as a confirmation or warning.
*   **Repetitive Realization:** Anya states the chip is “cut off” and then repeats the idea with "There’s nothing out there." and “No signal at all.” Condense this into one impactful statement.
*   **Subtext Opportunity (Data Chip):** The data chip's subtle assistance is a good start, but it feels a little isolated. Could this "deliberate assistance" be followed up with a quick glance between Anya and Leo, a shared look of understanding or suspicion?
