*   **Anya & Leo's Initial Reaction:** While "It's... impossible" and mirroring gestures are understandable, it feels a bit *too* passive. They’ve just discovered a city *inside* their observatory. A more active, questioning response – even a fragmented one – would feel more impactful. Consider something like: "Leo: What *is* that? Anya: It… it can’t be real.” or even just a shared, disbelieving laugh before the realization sets in.
*   **Old Man Tiber's Exposition:** The dialogue is heavily expositional. While necessary information, it's delivered as a monologue. Could some of this be revealed through Anya or Leo *asking* questions, prompting Tiber to share the information? Even a single question ("What do you mean, 'The Architects'?") would make it more dynamic.
*   **"Containment breach detected..." Voice:** The voice is a bit cliché. While functional, it lacks personality or a sense of urgency beyond the words themselves. Consider *how* it delivers the information – is it robotic? Panicked? Weary? Injecting that into the dialogue (even a slight vocal tic described in narration) would elevate it.
*   **Anya's Repeated Statements ("It's not just damaged… It's… cut off. Completely." "There’s nothing out there."):** This is repetitive. The first statement establishes the problem. The second is redundant. Show, don’t tell. Perhaps instead of *saying* there's nothing out there, she frantically cycles through frequencies, her face growing increasingly desperate as the screen remains blank.
*   **Subtext Opportunity - Data Chip:** The data chip is a crucial plot device. Anya and Leo are observing its fading light. There's a missed opportunity to have them *comment* on that fading light, to express concern or understanding of what it means for their situation. A simple line like, "Leo: The chip… it’s losing power. Anya: That can't be good." would add tension.
*   **Indirect Characterization:** Leo is largely silent. While not *wrong*, it makes him feel a bit passive. Even a small, worried glance at Anya, or a questioning look at Tiber, would establish his personality and relationship to the others.



Overall, the dialogue is functional but leans heavily towards exposition and lacks depth. It could benefit from more active responses, subtext, and opportunities to reveal character through interaction.
