## Scene 28 - The Static Between Worlds (Draft 4)

The weeks blurred into a rhythm of focused desperation. Each salvaged component, each re-routed connection within the Seeker, felt less like repair and more like a frantic prayer. The machine sputtered back to life in stages, a fragile heartbeat in the cavernous workshop. Finally, the main viewport flickered, resolving into static, then fractured images. Not the crisp, panoramic views of SimuLife I’d expected, but glitches. Fragments. A city skyline tearing apart like paper, a verdant forest dissolving into grey, faces… distorted, screaming silently.

The data stream wasn’t a flood, but a choked trickle, riddled with corruption. Complex algorithms, encrypted messages, technical schematics detailing… energy conduits, life support matrices, the very architecture of their world’s core. It felt less like accessing a system, and more like witnessing a dying mind.

“What is it?” Tiber’s voice, rough as shale, cut through the hum of the Seeker. He hadn’t offered help, hadn’t even lingered, but I caught him watching from the shadows, his face etched with a familiar skepticism.

“It’s… bad,” I said, my voice tight. “The system isn’t stable. It’s degrading. Fast.”

The images intensified, cycling through scenes of escalating chaos. Automated systems malfunctioning, transport networks collapsing, the vibrant hues of SimuLife bleeding into a monotonous grey. A recurring symbol – a stylized helix fractured at its center – pulsed within the data stream, a morbid heartbeat.  

“Degrading?” Tiber grunted. “Everything degrades, girl. That’s the nature of things.”

“No, this isn’t natural wear. Look.” I isolated a fragment, amplifying it on the viewport. A schematic of the central energy generator, the core of SimuLife, riddled with cascading failure points. “It’s not just failing, it’s *breaking*. Multiple systems are going critical simultaneously. It's… deliberate.”

The images shifted again, showing panicked citizens, their faces contorted with fear. A message, fragmented and distorted, repeated itself: *“System critical… power failing… life support compromised…”* Each repetition felt like a physical blow.  I pushed the Seeker’s processing power to its limit, filtering the noise, attempting to reconstruct a coherent narrative. 

“Deliberate?” Tiber scoffed, stepping closer, his gaze fixed on the viewport. “Who would sabotage their own world?”

I didn’t have an answer. But the data screamed it wasn’t an accident.  The sheer number of simultaneous failures, the targeted nature of the damage… it pointed to something far more sinister. I managed to isolate a clearer audio fragment, a desperate plea, barely audible beneath the static.

“…Elias Vance… research sector seven… core instability… not a malfunction… sabotage… they’re masking the source…” The voice cut out, replaced by a wave of white noise.

“Elias Vance?” I muttered, frantically searching the database. “A lead scientist. Specialized in energy systems. He disappeared five years ago.”

The images on the viewport intensified, the fractured helix symbol pulsing faster. The grey was spreading, consuming everything.  I felt a growing sense of dread, a suffocating weight pressing down on me.  This wasn’t just about fixing a machine. It was about saving a world.

“The system is failing, Tiber,” I said, my voice trembling. “And it’s happening faster now. We need to find out what Vance was working on, who silenced him, and what they’re trying to hide.”

He stared at the viewport, his face unreadable. The grey was almost complete, swallowing the last vestiges of color.  A single image remained, a distorted face, screaming silently.  

“Five years ago…” Tiber murmured, a flicker of something – concern? – crossing his features. “That was when the restrictions tightened. When they started shutting down research sectors.” 

The image flickered and died, replaced by a screen of pure static. The Seeker groaned, its systems straining under the load.  I felt a cold dread creep into my bones.  The static wasn’t just noise. It was a warning.
