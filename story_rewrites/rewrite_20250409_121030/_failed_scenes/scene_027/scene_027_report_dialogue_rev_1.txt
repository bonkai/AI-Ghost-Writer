*   **Repetitive phrasing:** The Seeker *constantly* speaks in fragmented sentences and repeats key words ("The Core…," "the network…," "much…"). While initially effective to convey damage/compromise, it becomes monotonous and detracts from the impact. Vary the sentence structure and phrasing *slightly* to avoid sounding like a broken record. Consider implying the damage through pauses and incomplete thoughts rather than explicitly stating it with every other word.
*   **On-the-nose exposition:** The Seeker *tells* us it's compromised; we don't *feel* it organically through its actions or dialogue. The constant listing of failing systems is an info-dump. Show, don't tell. For example, instead of "My operational capacity is…compromised," perhaps the Seeker struggles to complete a sentence, or its form glitches unexpectedly.
*   **Anya's lack of reaction:** Anya asks logical questions, but her internal state feels a bit flat. She’s encountering something *truly* alien and ancient, yet her responses are largely practical. Inject more wonder, fear, or skepticism into her dialogue. A small, reactive interjection ("What *is* this place?") would add depth.
*   **Subtext opportunity (Anya):** Anya lowers her rifle "slightly." This is a good start, but lean into that hesitation. A line like, "No energy spike...but that doesn't mean anything," would convey her professional caution *and* her growing unease.
*   **Subtext opportunity (Seeker):** The "fleeting impression of…loss" is a good idea, but it feels a little too neatly packaged. Instead of *telling* us it felt loss, show it through a momentary flicker in the sphere’s light, a distortion in the Seeker’s voice, or a subtle shift in the pressure against Anya’s mind.
*   **Seeker’s motivation:** The Seeker’s desperation is present, but its explanation of *why* preserving these records is important feels weak. “Much will be forgotten” is vague. Hint at *what* is being forgotten – a lost civilization, a crucial technology, a warning – to raise the stakes.
