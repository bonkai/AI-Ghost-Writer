The workshop throbbed with a desperate energy. Anya traced archaic power coupling schematics onto plast-steel, designs whispering of technology centuries older than SimuLife. Sparks flew as Kael relentlessly sharpened salvaged metal plates, the rhythmic clang a brutal counterpoint to the workshop’s failing hum. Across the room, Rori packed a heavy rucksack – dried rations, medical kits, a coiled water purification system disappearing within. She tested a hydration pack seal, glancing at Kael, her fingers worrying the plastic. “This ridge is exposed. Think this sealant will hold?”

Kael didn’t look up, driving the sharpening stone with savage force. “Always pack extra. You know the drill.” He tested the edge of a plate with his thumb, a flicker of concern crossing his face. “And thermal layers. Don’t skimp.”

Anya worked quickly, integrating salvaged SimuLife components into the ancient designs. Kael’s plates weren’t simply barriers; a subtle energy signature emanated from his hammer as he worked, layering a localized amplification field. Rori’s packing wasn’t random; coded markings etched onto her equipment revealed a complex logistical system.

Kael tested a reinforced plate, the metal ringing with a hollow echo. He glanced at Anya, a question in his eyes. “Almost ready?”

Anya didn’t return the look, her focus absolute. “Calibrating the energy flow. It’s delicate.” She activated a handheld device, emitting a low-frequency pulse. The workshop lights flickered, and she braced herself against the strain. “The System is already pushing back – power fluctuations are getting worse.”

Rori secured a water purification straw, then checked her climbing gear. She pulled a length of lum-rope from her pack, its faint glow illuminating her face. “Found a good supply. Should last the trek.” She traced a finger along the rope, revealing a faint, bioluminescent marker. “Elara says the ridge is a choke point. We’ll need every advantage.”

Elara, observing, adjusted a handheld scanner, her expression grim but determined. The scanner pulsed, mapping energy fluctuations and identifying weak points. Anomalies clustered on the display, centered on a distant ridge. “The System’s interference is spiking,” she murmured, her voice tight. “They’re anticipating our move. Less than six hours before full lockdown. And external comms are already throttling.” She didn’t meet anyone’s gaze, her attention solely on the cascading data.

Anya gathered the schematics, securing them in a waterproof pouch. She glanced at the security cameras, a fleeting awareness of being observed. Not recognition, but a prickling sensation on the back of her neck.

Kael finished reinforcing the wall, offering a curt nod to Anya. “Ready when you are.”

Elara deactivated her scanner, her expression hardening. “It’s time,” she said, her voice resolute. “We move at dawn. No delays.”

Anya turned, her gaze meeting Elara’s. She reached for the pack at her feet, the weight familiar and grounding. The workshop fell silent, save for the low hum of the power systems, now stuttering and strained. Anya didn’t lift the pack fully. Instead, she paused, her fingers tightening on the straps, and slowly, deliberately, activated a small, concealed disruptor, bathing the room in a field of static. A final, desperate gamble to mask their departure.
