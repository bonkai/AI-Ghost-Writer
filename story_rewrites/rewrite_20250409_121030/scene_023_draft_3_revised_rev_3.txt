The valley breathed a fragile peace. Dust motes danced in the late afternoon sun, illuminating the patchwork fields of nutrient crops. Anya traced the rim of her mug, the recycled ceramic warm against her fingers. It wasn’t the rich, dark clay of Old Earth, but it held heat. She missed the weight of it, the feel of something *real* in her hands.

“Signal’s stronger,” Tiber grumbled from the comm station, his voice rough as shale. “Definitely not atmospheric. Origin… sector Gamma-Nine. Deep within the Blasted Lands.”

Anya rose, her boots silent on the packed earth floor. The others – Kael, Elara, and Bram – were already gathered around the holographic display, the jagged peaks of Gamma-Nine glowing a sickly purple.

“Blasted Lands?” Kael’s brow furrowed. “That sector’s been dead for decades. Nothing but radiation and dust storms.”

“Exactly,” Tiber said, his gaze fixed on the readings. “Which makes this… unusual.”

The signal pulsed on the display, a faint, erratic heartbeat against the purple static.  It wasn’t a broadcast. It wasn’t a beacon. It was… something else.

“Could be a malfunction,” Elara suggested, her voice tight. “A ghost echo from the old war?”

“I’ve filtered for that,” Tiber countered. “This is new. And it’s growing stronger.”

Anya stepped closer, her hand hovering over the map. “Sector Gamma-Nine… that’s where the SimuLife facility was located.”

A hush fell over the group. SimuLife. The name tasted like ash in Anya’s mouth. The pre-Collapse corporation that had promised to solve the food crisis, only to unleash a genetically engineered blight that nearly wiped out humanity.

“That place is cursed,” Elara said, her voice barely a whisper.  She ran a hand across her brow, a faint line creasing her forehead.  “The Elders warned us never to go near it.”

“The Elders also believed the sky was falling,” Tiber retorted. “We need to know what’s generating this signal.”

“Knowing what’s *in* Gamma-Nine won’t change the fact that the land itself is poisoned,” Elara insisted. “It’s a waste of resources, and a risk we don’t need to take.”

Anya met Elara’s gaze, sensing a deeper fear beneath her resistance.  Elara’s mother had been a botanist, one of the few who’d worked tirelessly to contain the SimuLife blight. Anya remembered stories of her mother’s heartbreak, her desperate attempts to undo the damage.

“We can take precautions,” Anya said, her voice firm. “Protective suits, radiation shielding. We go in, scan the area, and come back. A quick reconnaissance mission.”

Kael studied the holographic display, his skepticism slowly giving way to curiosity.  “What kind of technology did SimuLife have?  Could this signal be some kind of automated system, still functioning after all these years?”

“Probably a trap,” Elara muttered.

“We’ve faced worse,” Anya said.  “I’m going. Kael, you’re with me. Your expertise in sensor technology will be invaluable.”

Kael nodded, a flicker of excitement in his eyes.  “Alright. I’m in.”

Bram shifted his weight, his expression reluctant.  “I still think it’s a fool’s errand,” he said, “but if you’re going, someone needs to pilot the scout vehicle.  I’ll go.”  He paused, then added, “I doubted it at first, but I see the necessity.”

Anya turned to Elara, hoping for some sign of acceptance.  Elara remained silent, her face pale.  “Just… be careful,” she finally said, her voice strained.

Anya nodded. “We will.”

---

The scout vehicle hummed with power as it navigated the desolate landscape of Gamma-Nine.  Dust devils danced across the cracked earth, obscuring the skeletal remains of the SimuLife facility.  Anya scanned the readings, her heart pounding in her chest.

“Signal’s getting stronger,” she reported over the comm. “It’s originating from the central research complex.”

As they approached the complex, a chilling silence descended.  The air crackled with static.  Anya felt a prickling sensation on the back of her neck.

Then, on the radar screen, a new reading appeared.  A faint, irregular pulse.

Anya didn’t mention it, but a knot of unease tightened in her chest. The pulse on the radar flickered again, a short, erratic burst, and then… silence. It felt less like a signal, and more like a heartbeat.  A slow, deliberate heartbeat, echoing from the ruins of a forgotten past.
