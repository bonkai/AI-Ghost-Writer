*   **Kael’s Reactions – Repetitive Physicality:** Kael’s tightening grip on the shock baton is mentioned *twice*. While tension is good, relying on the same physical cue repeatedly feels lazy. Consider varying his reactions – a shift in weight, a change in breathing, a verbal response.
*   **Anya's "I don't know" / "What does it mean?" - Overreliance on Direct Statement:** Anya states she doesn't know what the images are, and later asks “What does it mean?” These are opportunities for *showing* her confusion and dawning realization through internal monologue or more evocative descriptions of her physical/emotional response. Let the images *tell* the story, and let Anya *react* to them in a way that reveals her understanding (or lack thereof).
*   **“They’re worried” – Too On-the-Nose:** Anya stating “They’re worried” as she watches scientists with troubled expressions is redundant. The visual should convey that information.
*   **Monotone Data Delivery:** The machine reciting "Project: Genesis…" feels a bit flat. Consider adding a *slight* glitch or distortion to the voice, or a very brief pause before "Contingency: Simulation," to hint at the unsettling nature of the information.
*   **“What does it *mean*?” (Kael) – Another missed opportunity:** This is a key moment. Instead of Kael *asking* what it means, consider him stating a theory, a fear, or a half-formed understanding. Even a wrong guess would be more engaging.
*   **Anya’s Final Collapse - Could be more subtle:** While the weight of the revelation is significant, Anya’s immediate collapse feels a bit dramatic. Perhaps a slower descent into realization, a growing sense of unease, and *then* the physical reaction would feel more earned.
