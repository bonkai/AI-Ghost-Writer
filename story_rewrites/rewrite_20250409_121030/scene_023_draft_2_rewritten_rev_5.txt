The valley breathed a fragile peace. Dust motes danced in the late afternoon sun, illuminating the patchwork fields of nutrient crops. Anya traced the rim of her mug, the recycled ceramic warm against her fingers. A tremor ran through the floor, almost imperceptible. 

“Signal’s stronger,” Tiber grumbled from the comm station. “Definitely not atmospheric. Origin… sector Gamma-Nine. Deep within the Blasted Lands.”

Anya rose, her hand instinctively reaching for the map table. Kael was already there, studying the holographic projection. “Gamma-Nine… that’s where the SimuLife facility was located.”

A hush fell over the group. Anya remembered the stories – SimuLife, the corporation that promised salvation and delivered ruin. 

“We can take precautions,” Anya said, her voice firm. “Protective suits, shielding. A quick reconnaissance.”

Kael studied the display. “What kind of technology did SimuLife have? Could this be automated?”

“Probably a trap,” Elara insisted, her voice tight. “My mother spent years cleaning up their mess. Poisoned ground, Anya. Best left undisturbed.”

Anya met Elara’s gaze, sensing the deeper roots of her resistance. She remembered the exhaustion that had claimed Elara’s mother. 

“We *can* take precautions,” Anya repeated, softening her tone. “It’s a risk, but ignoring it could be bigger.”

Kael nodded. “Alright. I’m in. I want to see what they were working on.”

Bram shifted his weight. “Fool’s errand,” he said, “but if you’re going, someone needs to pilot the scout vehicle. I’ll go.”

Anya turned to Elara. Elara remained silent, another tremor running through the floor. She flinched. “Just… be careful,” she finally said, her voice strained. “And don’t stay long.”

Anya nodded. “We will.”

---

The scout vehicle hummed as it navigated the desolate landscape of Gamma-Nine. Dust devils danced across the cracked earth, obscuring the skeletal remains of the SimuLife facility. Anya scanned the readings, her heart pounding. The air tasted metallic. 

“Signal’s getting stronger,” Anya reported. “Originating from the central research complex.” 

As they approached, a chilling silence descended. Anya felt a prickling sensation. 

Then, on the radar screen, a new reading appeared – a faint, irregular pulse. It wasn’t clean, but chaotic, almost… organic.

“Kael, did you see that?” Anya asked.

Kael’s eyes were fixed on his sensors. “It’s fluctuating,” he said, his voice barely a whisper. “The energy signature… it’s changing. And it’s… rhythmic.” He looked up, his face pale. “It’s mimicking a cardiac rhythm.” 

Another tremor shook the vehicle. Anya glanced at Kael. "Did you feel that?"

Kael didn't answer, his gaze locked on the readings. The pulse on the radar flickered again, and then… silence. It felt less like a signal, and more like a heartbeat. A slow, deliberate heartbeat, echoing from the ruins.
